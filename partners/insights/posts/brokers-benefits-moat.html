Why retirement was off the table — until now

Brokers historically steered clear of workplace retirement plans for one reason: the administrative and compliance load. Manual payroll runs, annual testing, government filings, fiduciary exposure — none of it was worth bolting onto a P&C relationship. The Pooled Employer Plan removes that load by design.

How the fiduciary offload actually works

Pooled Employer Plans were created by the SECURE Act of 2019 and have been available since January 1, 2021. They let unrelated small employers join a single plan run by a registered Pooled Plan Provider. Two pieces matter for your client:

  • The Pooled Plan Provider serves as the plan's named fiduciary and its ERISA 3(16) plan administrator — taking on most of the administrative and compliance duties the employer would otherwise carry.
  • A 3(38) investment manager takes discretionary responsibility for the investment lineup. Per Department of Labor guidance, the employer's fiduciary exposure is minimized when the provider assumes full responsibility for selecting and monitoring that manager.

One honest caveat worth saying out loud to clients, because it builds trust: the employer never sheds every duty — it keeps a residual responsibility to prudently select and monitor the provider. That's a small, defined job replacing a large, open-ended one. Adoption reflects the appeal: the DOL counted 142 registered providers by the end of 2023, and pooled plans had grown to roughly 618,000 participants.
