The year-end trap hiding in a "matching" 401(k)

ThinkNirvana · June 27, 2026 · 4 min read

You read corporate returns and plan filings all day. So you've seen this pattern: a growing small business sets up a traditional matching 401(k), the owners and a few key people max out, and rank-and-file participation stays thin. That imbalance is exactly what annual nondiscrimination testing is built to catch — and when it fails, the bill lands on the people you advise.

How the trap springs

Every traditional 401(k) must pass two yearly tests — the Actual Deferral Percentage (ADP) and Actual Contribution Percentage (ACP) tests. They compare what highly compensated employees defer against everyone else. For the 2026 plan year, a highly compensated employee is generally anyone who earned more than $160,000 in 2025, or owns more than 5% of the business.

When the plan tilts too far toward those employees, it fails. The fixes aren't pleasant:

  • Excess contributions get refunded to the highly compensated employees — and that refund is taxable to them, reported on a Form 1099-R.
  • Or the employer funds corrective contributions (QNECs) to everyone else to pull the average up.
  • Miss the correction window — 2½ months after plan year-end, so March 15 for calendar-year plans — and the employer owes a 10% excise tax on the excess.
  • Leave it uncorrected past 12 months and the plan can lose its tax-qualified status entirely.
A failed test is the kind of surprise that lands in your inbox in March, after the return is already on your desk.

The cleaner design

A safe-harbor plan design is generally exempt from ADP/ACP testing altogether — the testing headache simply goes away.
